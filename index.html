About

Job Market Paper

Job Market Paper

Digital Government and Sovereign Default Risk: Tax Enforcement, Revenue Diversion, and Fiscal Capacity

This paper analyzes how government adoption of digital technologies shapes sovereign default risk through its effect on tax enforcement and revenue diversion. Using cross-country data for 46 economies, I find that higher digital adoption is associated with significantly lower sovereign spreads and higher government tax revenue, robust to controls for institutional quality and corruption. Exploiting a novel database of digital fiscal reforms, constructed by applying natural language processing to PEFA assessments to identify reform types and implementation dates, I show that customs automation, treasury digitization, and IFMIS adoption raise tax revenue following implementation. A sovereign default model in which tax administrators divert revenue and digital capacity raises both the probability of detecting diversion and the penalty conditional on detection, aligns with these results. Calibrated via simulated method of moments, the quantitative analysis suggests that digital investment substantially lowers spreads, curbs diversion, and generates large welfare gains, particularly for highly indebted governments.
